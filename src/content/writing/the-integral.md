**Diamonds and Trains**  
Anna Fisher Lopez

*“Common sense is just a name for the way we’re used to thinking.”*   
*– Rebecca Stead*

I remember the first argument I had with my best friend in middle school. I was wearing leggings under my skinny jeans to protect from the bite of the January air, and we had just passed the bakery that marked the first landmark on our mile-long walk to school. 

“Of course your five-year-old self doesn’t exist\! That’s stupid.” Delia was more frustrated than I’d ever seen her. 

“But it does\!” I had just read a book called *When You Reach Me*, Rebecca Stead’s award-winning novel about a young girl who discovers the meaning of friendship and loss amidst a background of time travel and mystery-solving. As a result, I had adopted grandiose ideas about the meaning of time. “Just because we aren’t five years old *now* doesn’t mean she doesn’t exist\!” 

Delia was silent. I felt the need to go on, to defend myself. “It’s like diamonds going around a really big ring,” I said, shamelessly plagiarizing a metaphor I learned from the book. “Each diamond is a moment in time. We can only hop from diamond to diamond, always going in one direction. But the other diamonds are still there, we just can’t be on more than one at a time\!”

“Whatever.” Lately, she’d been saying that word all the time. “I don’t want to talk about it anymore.”

But I was desperate to convince her. I needed someone else to help me understand what I was trying to explain. “It’s like train tracks\!” I could sense her irritation that I hadn’t dropped it. “We’re on a train that can only go one way, but the whole track, the whole timeline, exists at once.” I was talking faster and faster, and I knew she wasn’t listening anymore. “It’s just like any other dimension, forward and back, left and right, up and down, it’s just that with time, we don’t have any control over the direction we travel\!” 

“STOP\!” she yelled. We were emotional creatures back then.

Not too long after that first argument, I became engrossed in “Doctor Who,” the  TV show that has captured the hearts of science fiction nerds for decades since it first aired in 1963\. Now, it was 2015, which meant that if you were a fan of Doctor Who there were plenty of Tumblr profiles, Pinterest boards, and Instagram fan accounts run by eyeshadowed, dark-haired, cat-ear-wearing girls full of Doctor Who quotes about time. I was their target audience. 

“*Look,*” I said, shoving my phone in front of Delia so she would *have* to read the Doctor Who quote  displayed in big block letters on the screen: 

“People assume that time is a strict progression from cause to effect, but actually from a   
non-linear, non-subjective viewpoint, it's more like a big ball of wibbly-wobbly,   
timey-wimey stuff.” 

She stared at me with a blank indifference. “Non-linear\!” I emphasized. “Look, he’s saying time is not actually the straight line we think it is\!” I was contradicting my earlier diamond ring example, I knew, but I had to convince her that something *weird* was going on, that there was something worth interrogating. 

She sighed. “It literally doesn’t matter.”

As I grew older, my suspicions slowly became confirmed. Talk of the “space-time continuum” began to appear in books I was reading, books written by scientists this time, not fiction writers. I learned that space and time really *were* different versions of the same thing, and I wanted to tell Delia, but she had a boyfriend now, and I did too, and we only really saw each other at lunch, and even then just barely, and maybe it wasn’t worth bringing up old conflicts just for the sake of being vaguely proven right.

Finally, during my senior spring of high school, the secret was revealed to me. Over the course of a semester-long physics class, my teacher explained the concept of special relativity in the following way: 

Imagine that two people are throwing a ball back and forth on a train. If those two people were to measure the speed of the ball using, say,  a yardstick and a stopwatch, it would be traveling at a perfectly reasonable velocity, maybe 15-20 miles an hour. Now imagine a third person standing on the side of the tracks, watching the train go by. If that person looks through the window and measures the speed of the ball moving through the air, they’ll observe it moving much faster as the train zips by, something like 60-100 miles an hour. This is because the person on the tracks sees the velocity of the ball in addition to the velocity of the train, while the people on the train only notice the velocity of the ball. The point is this: two people traveling at two different speeds will get two different measurements of the velocity of the same object, and both of them are correct. Velocity is measured *relative* to the speed of the observer. Every time a measurement is done, we must take note of the velocity, or *v,* of the observer – the people on the train would have a *v* equal to the speed of the train, while the person on the tracks has a *v* of zero. 

Physicists say that people moving at different velocities are in different “frames” – the people on the train are in a “frame” which is moving faster than the “frame” of the person standing on the side of the tracks. An observer’s “frame” matters because it changes the way that they measure not only velocity, but other fundamental constants like length, energy, and time. These changes are dictated by the following equation:

𝛾  \=  11-v2c2

This equation may look complicated, but its explanation is simple and its meaning is powerful. The symbol on the left hand side of this equation is called “gamma,” and the expression on the right produces a number, something like 1.5 or 8\. So if we evaluate this equation we’d get 𝛾  \= 1.5, or maybe 𝛾  \= 8\. The *v* in this equation is the same *v* we discussed before – the velocity of the frame of the observer that we care about. And *c* just stands for the number 300,000,000, which is the speed of light in meters per second. 

Gamma (𝛾) is the factor by which length, energy, time, and other fundamental physics quantities are multiplied when measured by someone in a moving frame relative to how they would be measured in a stationary frame. For example, a clock with a minute hand that makes a full circle once an hour for you might take 1.5, or 8 hours to do the same when I’m looking at it, if I’m in a different frame with some velocity *v*. This is a powerful concept: the length of time representing one hour to you is different from the length of time representing one hour to me.   

You may be thinking: why does time work at all, if it’s different for everyone? Why don’t my clocks run slower on airplanes than they do on the ground? The answer is that in order for gamma to be big enough to matter, you have to be going *really, really* fast. When *v* is much slower than the speed of light, like it is on a train, airplane, or even the best spaceships we can build, gamma is very close to 1\. That means that a minute hand which takes an hour to go around in a stationary frame might take 1.0000000000001 (roughly) hours to go around in a Space Shuttle’s frame. Gamma only becomes big enough to notice if the observer is traveling at speeds close to the speed of light, which is not within humanity’s technological capabilities. The only things we can measure that reach those speeds are subatomic particles. And therefore, most of us slow-moving humans never question the idea that time is the same for everyone.

*“We have to keep moving—there’s no choice”*  
*– Rebecca Stead*

The other day a photo memory popped up on my photos app: “4 years ago today.” It was a selfie of me and my dog lying on the floor of my living room, the brown rug collecting the dust of a family of four that had been stuck at home together for almost two months, and would be stuck there for many more. I scrolled through every photo and video I took during May of 2020 for the first time since it had all happened. There was me, recording myself playing a new piano song I’d learned with all of my new free time. A picture of the inside of a Costco, the first time I’d gone anywhere outside my home in months. A video I submitted for a school assignment of myself talking about what my goals were for the month of May, and how I would achieve them. And there were scanned photos of my math homework, my statistics homework, my physics homework, to be uploaded to Canvas for submission, done precisely and thoughtfully because there was nothing better to be done. 

The person I was then is as full and as real and as alive as the person I am now, but there is no way for me to access her. She is the Me Then, and I am the Me Now. 

As I travel through time, I leave behind a trail of Me Thens, and the Me Now shifts, moment by moment, to the line of *future* Me Thens that is laid out before me. We are sentenced by an unnameable force to go only forward, to occupy just one point at a time, to continuously abandon each part of ourselves except the one we currently occupy. It’s somewhat tragic, that the only self that we can truly know is the self in the present moment, for as time goes we can only use memories and feelings, biased by the Me Now, to try and understand the Me Then. My past self is reduced to whatever she decided to record for future posterity. 

There are certainly things that are integral to ourselves as people, things that we maintain throughout our passage through time, things that we think of as being a product of our past selves but perhaps are also a product of our future in ways that we cannot understand. For whatever reason, we can only access the Me Thens that lie behind us, and not the ones that lie ahead, but who’s to say that the future Me Thens are unimportant? The things that are integral to us could be a product of every moment we have had and *will* have. And isn’t that what *integral* means – the sum of values for every infinitesimally small piece along a path.

Physics can’t explain why we are three-dimensional beings and have no control of our travel through the fourth, but physics does know that there are many, many more dimensions beyond the three we have mastered. Just like other animals can see colors which we can’t perceive, it is possible that other beings could have a comprehensive understanding of that fourth dimension, time, which is a mystery to us. 

It’s a running joke now, between Delia and I, the argument that our five-year-old selves still exist. My conviction is stronger than ever, and she remains stubbornly skeptical, but our friendship has stood the test of time. We know that there are more important things running throughout our past and future. 

And somewhere out there, my middle school self is getting frustrated with her, and my high school self is falling in love with physics, and the Me Now looks back on them and smiles before turning forward, because although I know I’ll be gone in an instant, I’m happy so far with the way the integral has turned out.
